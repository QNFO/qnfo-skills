## 2.1 Introduction: From Trees to Buildings

Chapter 1 established that the $p$-adic integers $\mathbb{Z}_p$ form a compact ultrametric space whose ball structure is a regular $p$-ary tree. This chapter generalizes that tree to the full **Bruhat-Tits building** — a polysimplicial complex that encodes the representation theory and geometry of reductive algebraic groups over non-Archimedean local fields [established].

The Bruhat-Tits building is the geometric object that bridges Chapter 1's abstract ultrametric spaces with the physical applications of the Kepler Program (adelic QEC, tree-depth time). Understanding buildings is essential for grasping *why* ultrametric structures appear in quantum error correction and cosmology [my conjecture].

## 2.2 BN-Pairs and Tits Systems

### 2.2.1 Definition

A **BN-pair** (or Tits system) for a group $G$ consists of subgroups $B$ (Borel) and $N$ (normalizer of a maximal torus) such that:

1. $G = \langle B, N \rangle$
2. $T = B \cap N \trianglelefteq N$
3. $W = N/T$ (the Weyl group) is generated by a set $S$ of involutions
4. $sBw \subseteq BwB \cup BswB$ for all $s \in S, w \in W$
5. $sBs \neq B$ for all $s \in S$

The Weyl group $W$ with generating set $S$ forms a **Coxeter system** [established].

### 2.2.2 The Coxeter Complex

The Coxeter group $(W, S)$ defines a simplicial complex $\Sigma(W, S)$ — the **Coxeter complex** — whose simplices are the cosets $wW_J$ for $J \subseteq S$, where $W_J = \langle J \rangle$. The maximal simplices correspond to cosets of the trivial subgroup, i.e., elements of $W$, and the codimension-1 faces correspond to cosets of rank-1 parabolic subgroups $W_{\{s\}}$ [established].

### 2.2.3 Example: $\mathrm{GL}(n, k)$

For $G = \mathrm{GL}(n, k)$ over a field $k$:
- $B$ = upper triangular matrices (Borel subgroup)
- $N$ = monomial matrices (one non-zero entry per row/column)
- $T = B \cap N$ = diagonal matrices (maximal torus)
- $W = N/T \cong S_n$ (symmetric group)
- $S$ = adjacent transpositions $(i, i+1)$

The Coxeter complex for $S_n$ is the barycentric subdivision of an $(n-1)$-simplex [established].

## 2.3 Affine Buildings

### 2.3.1 From Spherical to Affine

When $G$ is a reductive group over a **non-Archimedean local field** $K$ (e.g., $\mathbb{Q}_p$ or $\mathbb{F}_p((t))$), the group $G(K)$ admits an **affine BN-pair**. The resulting building is an **affine building** — a polysimplicial complex with apartments that are affine Coxeter complexes (Euclidean tilings) [established].

### 2.3.2 The Affine Weyl Group

For $G = \mathrm{SL}(n, \mathbb{Q}_p)$, the affine Weyl group is:

$$W_{\text{aff}} = W \ltimes Q^\vee$$

where $W = S_n$ is the spherical Weyl group and $Q^\vee$ is the coroot lattice. The affine Weyl group is a Coxeter group generated by $S \cup \{s_0\}$ where $s_0$ is the affine reflection corresponding to the highest root [established].

### 2.3.3 The Building for $\mathrm{SL}(2, \mathbb{Q}_p)$

The building for $\mathrm{SL}(2, \mathbb{Q}_p)$ is a **regular $(p+1)$-regular tree** — the Bruhat-Tits tree introduced in Chapter 1, §1.5. The vertices correspond to homothety classes of lattices in $\mathbb{Q}_p^2$, and the edges correspond to inclusion of lattices with index $p$ [established].

**Key Fact:** The Bruhat-Tits tree for $\mathrm{SL}(2, \mathbb{Q}_p)$ is the 1-dimensional affine building of type $\tilde{A}_1$. It has:
- Valency $p+1$ at every vertex
- Apartments that are bi-infinite geodesics
- An action of $\mathrm{SL}(2, \mathbb{Q}_p)$ by simplicial automorphisms

## 2.4 Structure Theory of Affine Buildings

### 2.4.1 Apartments

An **apartment** in an affine building is a maximal flat — a subcomplex isomorphic to the affine Coxeter complex. Every apartment is a Euclidean space tiled by a Coxeter arrangement (the affine Weyl group action). For the Bruhat-Tits tree ($\tilde{A}_1$), an apartment is simply a bi-infinite path — a 1-dimensional Euclidean space tiled by unit intervals [established].

### 2.4.2 Retractions

For any chamber $C$ and apartment $A$ containing $C$, there is a unique **retraction** $\rho_{A, C}: \Delta \to A$ that maps the building onto the apartment, preserving distances and fixing $C$ pointwise. This retraction property is the geometric analogue of the Cartan decomposition $G = BWB$ [established].

### 2.4.3 Distance and the $W$-valued Metric

The building $\Delta$ admits a $W$-valued distance function (the **Weyl distance**):

$$\delta(C, D) = w \in W$$

where $w$ measures the relative position of chambers $C$ and $D$. For the Bruhat-Tits tree, this reduces to the graph distance: $\delta(v, w) = d(v, w) \in \mathbb{N}$ [established].

## 2.5 Buildings as Classifying Spaces

### 2.5.1 Parahoric Subgroups

A **parahoric subgroup** of $G(K)$ is the stabilizer of a facet in the Bruhat-Tits building. These are the natural analogues of parabolic subgroups in the non-Archimedean setting. The building $\Delta$ serves as the **classifying space** for parahoric subgroups:

$$\{\text{parahoric subgroups of } G(K)\} \longleftrightarrow \{\text{facets of } \Delta\}$$

This correspondence is fundamental: the geometry of the building encodes the entire subgroup structure of $G(K)$ [established].

### 2.5.2 The Holographic Principle of Buildings

Every geometric property of the building corresponds to an algebraic property of the group. This "holographic" correspondence is the deep insight of Bruhat-Tits theory [established]:

| Geometry of $\Delta$ | Algebra of $G(K)$ |
|:---------------------|:------------------|
| Chambers | Iwahori subgroups |
| Vertices | Maximal parahoric subgroups |
| Apartments | Maximal split tori |
| Geodesics | Cartan decomposition |
| Retractions | Iwasawa decomposition |

## 2.6 Ultrametric Trees as Rank-1 Buildings

For a rank-1 group (e.g., $\mathrm{SL}(2)$), the Bruhat-Tits building is a tree. This tree is not just any tree — it is an **ultrametric tree**: the graph distance $d(v, w)$ satisfies the strong triangle inequality when measured from any fixed root vertex [established].

### 2.6.1 Tree Metrics are Ultrametrics

**Theorem 2.1:** Let $T$ be a rooted tree with edge lengths. Define the distance $d(x, y)$ as the length of the unique path between leaves $x$ and $y$ in the subtree below their least common ancestor. Then $d$ is an ultrametric.

**Proof:** For any three leaves $x, y, z$, let $u = \text{lca}(x, y)$, $v = \text{lca}(x, z)$. Since $T$ is a tree, either $u$ is an ancestor of $v$ or vice versa. If $u$ is above $v$, then $d(x, z) = d(y, z)$ and $d(x, y) \leq d(x, z) = \max\{d(x, z), d(y, z)\}$. The other case is symmetric. $\square$ [established]

This theorem is the geometric foundation of the entire Kepler Program: **all Bruhat-Tits buildings are ultrametric spaces in a generalized sense**, and their rank-1 substructures are literal ultrametric trees. The adelic QEC architecture (Kepler Phase 2) exploits precisely this tree structure.

## 2.7 Connection to the Kepler Program

### 2.7.1 Adelic QEC and Building Geometry

In Kepler Phase 2, the adelic QEC architecture maps logical qubits to chambers in the Bruhat-Tits building and error syndromes to Weyl distances between chambers. This mapping is not metaphorical — it is a mathematical isomorphism between:
- Quantum error-correcting codes over $\bigotimes_p \mathbb{Q}_p$
- Simplicial complexes in the Bruhat-Tits building for $\prod_p \mathrm{SL}(2, \mathbb{Q}_p)$

The "adelic" nature comes from the fact that the building factorizes over primes: $\Delta(\prod_p G(\mathbb{Q}_p)) = \prod_p \Delta(G(\mathbb{Q}_p))$, a product of Bruhat-Tits trees [established].

### 2.7.2 Tree-Depth Time

In Kepler Phase 8, time is modeled as depth in an ultrametric tree. The Bruhat-Tits tree provides the geometric realization: causal ordering corresponds to ancestor-descendant relationships in the tree, and the arrow of time is the direction of increasing depth [my conjecture].
