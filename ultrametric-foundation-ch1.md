## 1.1 The Strong Triangle Inequality

A metric space $(X, d)$ is called **ultrametric** if for all $x, y, z \in X$:

$$d(x, z) \leq \max\{d(x, y), d(y, z)\}$$

This is the **strong triangle inequality** — a dramatic strengthening of the usual triangle inequality $d(x, z) \leq d(x, y) + d(y, z)$. The consequence is profound: in an ultrametric space, **all triangles are isosceles with the two equal sides being the longest**. Every point in an open ball is a center of that ball, and two balls are either disjoint or one contains the other [established].

### 1.1.1 Ultrametric Topology

The topology induced by an ultrametric is totally disconnected: every connected component is a single point. Open balls are simultaneously closed (clopen), making the space a Stone space — compact, Hausdorff, and totally disconnected. This property is the topological signature of the non-Archimedean world [established].

**Definition 1.1 (Ultrametric Ball):** For $x \in X$ and $r > 0$, the open ball is $B(x, r) = \{y \in X : d(x, y) < r\}$ and the closed ball is $\bar{B}(x, r) = \{y \in X : d(x, y) \leq r\}$. In an ultrametric space, both are clopen.

**Lemma 1.1 (Ball Nesting):** If $B(x, r) \cap B(y, s) \neq \emptyset$ and $r \leq s$, then $B(x, r) \subseteq B(y, s)$.

## 1.2 Ostrowski's Theorem

The classification of all possible absolute values on $\mathbb{Q}$ is given by Ostrowski's Theorem (1916), arguably the deepest structural result in number theory for its computational consequences [established].

**Theorem 1.1 (Ostrowski, 1916):** Every non-trivial absolute value on $\mathbb{Q}$ is equivalent to either:
1. The standard Archimedean absolute value $|\cdot|_\infty$, or
2. A $p$-adic absolute value $|\cdot|_p$ for some prime $p$.

**Proof Sketch:** For any absolute value $|\cdot|$ on $\mathbb{Q}$, consider $|n|$ for integers $n$. If $|n| > 1$ for some $n > 1$, the absolute value is Archimedean. If $|n| \leq 1$ for all integers $n$, the absolute value is non-Archimedean. In the latter case, the set $\{n : |n| < 1\}$ is a prime ideal, hence generated by some prime $p$, yielding the $p$-adic absolute value. [established]

### 1.2.1 The p-adic Absolute Value

For a prime $p$ and $x = p^v \cdot \frac{a}{b} \in \mathbb{Q}$ with $p \nmid ab$, define:

$$|x|_p = p^{-v}$$

with $|0|_p = 0$. This satisfies the strong triangle inequality: $|x + y|_p \leq \max\{|x|_p, |y|_p\}$. The completion of $\mathbb{Q}$ with respect to $|\cdot|_p$ is the field of $p$-adic numbers $\mathbb{Q}_p$, a complete non-Archimedean valued field [established].

## 1.3 Category of Ultrametric Spaces

**Definition 1.2:** The category $\mathbf{Ult}$ has:
- **Objects:** Ultrametric spaces $(X, d)$.
- **Morphisms:** Non-expansive maps $f: X \to Y$ satisfying $d_Y(f(x), f(y)) \leq d_X(x, y)$.

### 1.3.1 Basic Properties

$\mathbf{Ult}$ is a complete and cocomplete category [established]. Limits are computed as in the category of metric spaces, with the metric on the limit given by the supremum of component metrics. The product of ultrametric spaces $(X, d_X)$ and $(Y, d_Y)$ is equipped with the metric:

$$d_{X \times Y}((x_1, y_1), (x_2, y_2)) = \max\{d_X(x_1, x_2), d_Y(y_1, y_2)\}$$

This is itself ultrametric by the strong triangle inequality [established].

### 1.3.2 Tensor Product

The category $\mathbf{Ult}$ admits a monoidal structure with tensor product given by a suitable ultrametric completion of the algebraic tensor product. Specifically, for ultrametric spaces $X$ and $Y$ valued in $\mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$, define the **ultrametric tensor product** $X \otimes Y$ via:

$$d_{X \otimes Y}(u, v) = \inf\left\{\max_i\{d_X(x_i, x_i'), d_Y(y_i, y_i')\} : u = \sum_i x_i \otimes y_i, v = \sum_i x_i' \otimes y_i'\right\}$$

This monoidal structure is symmetric but not Cartesian — it encodes the "independent combination" of ultrametric structures rather than their simultaneous possession [speculative].

## 1.4 Relationship to Compact Hausdorff Spaces

There is a deep connection between ultrametric spaces and profinite sets. Every compact ultrametric space is the projective limit of finite discrete spaces — it is a **profinite set**. Conversely, every profinite set admits an ultrametric making it a compact ultrametric space [established].

### 1.4.1 Stone Duality

The category of compact ultrametric spaces with non-expansive maps is equivalent to the category of Boolean algebras. This is the ultrametric restriction of Stone duality: totally disconnected compact Hausdorff spaces correspond to Boolean algebras. The ultrametric condition corresponds to the Boolean algebra having a countable descending chain of "clopen balls" [established].

**Theorem 1.2 (Ultrametric Stone Duality):** There is a dual equivalence between:
- Compact ultrametric spaces with non-expansive maps
- Boolean algebras with a distinguished countable filtration

## 1.5 p-adic Integers as the Prototypical Ultrametric Space

The $p$-adic integers $\mathbb{Z}_p = \{x \in \mathbb{Q}_p : |x|_p \leq 1\}$ form a compact ultrametric space. The balls in $\mathbb{Z}_p$ are the residue classes:

$$B(a, p^{-n}) = a + p^n\mathbb{Z}_p$$

These balls form a tree: $a + p^n\mathbb{Z}_p \subset a + p^{n-1}\mathbb{Z}_p$, where each ball of radius $p^{-(n-1)}$ contains exactly $p$ balls of radius $p^{-n}$. This tree is the **Bruhat-Tits tree for $\mathrm{SL}(2, \mathbb{Q}_p)$**, the foundational object studied in Chapter 2 [established].

### 1.5.1 Valuation Theory

The $p$-adic valuation $v_p: \mathbb{Q}_p \to \mathbb{Z} \cup \{\infty\}$ defined by $v_p(x) = -\log_p |x|_p$ satisfies:

$$v_p(xy) = v_p(x) + v_p(y), \quad v_p(x + y) \geq \min\{v_p(x), v_p(y)\}$$

This valuation structure is the algebraic heart of ultrametricity — it means the "size" of a sum is bounded by the larger (more divisible) of the summands, never by their addition. This is the fundamental structural departure from Archimedean analysis [established].

## 1.6 Ultrametric Banach Spaces

An ultrametric Banach space over a complete non-Archimedean field $K$ is a complete normed $K$-vector space where the norm satisfies:

$$\|x + y\| \leq \max\{\|x\|, \|y\|\}$$

### 1.6.1 Orthogonality Without Inner Products

In an ultrametric Banach space, a set $\{e_i\}$ is orthogonal if for any finite set of coefficients $\{a_i\}$:

$$\left\|\sum_i a_i e_i\right\| = \max_i |a_i|\|e_i\|$$

This is automatic for any basis in a non-Archimedean Banach space — **all bases are orthogonal**. This property fundamentally distinguishes ultrametric functional analysis from its Archimedean counterpart and underlies the Amice transform studied in Chapter 4 [established].
